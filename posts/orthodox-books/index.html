Orthodox Books

January 21, 2019

Here’s a listing of books I’ve read concerning some aspect of the Orthodox Church. This list will be added to, and modified, as needed.

Chadwick, Henry. East and West: The Making of a Rift in the Church. Oxford University Press, 2005.

Henry Chadwick was a recognized authority on the history of the early Church. He was an Anglican, which probably lends some author neutrality to this study of the origins and causes of the division between the East and West. Among the interesting topics covered are the differences that had already emerged by the first few ecumenical councils, the complex nature of Photius and the surrounding schism, and the proceedings of the Council of Florence, in which all but one of the Orthodox delegates caved to the Pope and signed the proceedings (but which was to be rejected by the Orthodox Church, as a whole).

Damick, Andrew Stephen. Orthodoxy and Heterodoxy: Finding the Way to Christ in a Complicated Religious Landscape (Revised Edition). Ancient Faith Publishing, 2017.

Each major faith’s views are presented in contrast to Orthodox Christianity, and both differences and similarities are noted. Father Damick must have done a formidable amount of research for this book, because his presentation of each faith is extensive. He devotes an entire section to Roman Catholicism, and moves on to treat each of the classes of Protestant sects (Reformed, Pentacostal, Evangelical, etc.). Non-Christian religions are given much briefer treatment, but still get a section. Like the other books in this list, the author’s tone is not militant or emotional, but also cuts right to the chase and lays out how he sees things. You very well might not agree with everything Damick says, but the direct nature of his writing means you’ll know exactly where you do and do not agree, and you can go from there.

Dvornik, Francis. The Photian Schism: History and Legend. Cambridge University Press, 1948.

Even though it was written in 1948, Dvornik’s book remains the definitive study of the events surrounding Photius’ patriarchate and the schism with Rome. Dvornik was a Roman Catholic, yet his book concludes with the assertion that Photius has been grossly misrepresented by the Catholic Church in the centuries since the schism. A particularly interesting fact is that the Western Church considered Photius a saint and accepted the second council of Constantinople as canonical for a few centuries, until the Gregorian Reforms backtracked in the or 11th century.

Palmer, G.E.H., Sherrard, Philip, and Ware, Timothy. The Philokalia: Volume 1. Farrar, Straus, and Giroux, 1983.

This compilation of writings of early Eastern Church fathers is an excellent portal to the richness of Eastern spirituality. I continue to be struck by the focus on mindfulness and getting the passions under control, rather than on the mystical or things that we cannot control. Get your interior house in order, and let others deal with their own issues.

Ware, Timothy. The Orthodox Church: An Introduction to Eastern Christianity. Penguin Books, 2015.

Bishop Ware’s book is an exhaustive reference on all things Orthodox. Whether it’s some aspect of Church history, one of the Mysteries (Sacraments), or devotions and fasting, this book likely addresses it (though one may want to search for more specialized books for added detail on a given topic). Bishop Ware was educated at Oxford and teaches in Bath, and I’ve found his approach more scholarly than emotional.
