What Makes a Good Book?

December 19, 2018

Go to Barnes and Noble (or if you’re lazy, Amazon) and for any given topic you’ll find so many books with intriguing titles and enticing cover art. Which one to choose?

Unfortunately, many of the bestseller books in any given category are written, not by experts in the field, but by someone with strong opinions and an engaging writing style. Here’s an example, thankfully not on any bestseller list I’m aware of, but popular enough that it was assigned reading in a men’s group at my dad’s church. The author, Anthony Esolen, is listed as the author of 6 books on Amazon. Out of the Ashes is a book filled with poorly-formed philosophical conjectures supported by a host of reductio ad absurdam arguments. There’s a good reason for this: the author has a Ph.D., but in English! I have reason to believe his translations on Dante are decent enough, but he shouldn’t be automatically held credible for excursions into other fields.

I’ve come up with a list of things I look for to get a quick idea of whether a book is worth perusing:

  1. What are the author’s credentials?
    • Does he or she have an advanced degree in this particular field?
    • Does he or she hold a position in the field at a reputable institution?
  2. When was the book written?
    • This is particularly important for history books, because newer books will tend to make use of all the knowledge gained in the past, plus the latest research.
    • I’ve noticed that some books written on religious history topics in the 19th century are a bit, um, polarized/biased, so I tend to especially try to steer clear of these.
  3. Is the breadth of the topic reasonable?
    • Books written on too narrow of a topic often expect a lot more background knowledge from the reader.
    • Books written on a very broad topic might cover each item too superficially to be really interesting.
  4. What are the alternatives?
    • Sometimes I’ll end up choosing a book that falls short on one or more of these standards, just because there isn’t anything better available from whatever library I happen to be at. This is why I almost exclusively get books from the two college libraries in my area instead of public libraries.

Books that follow these guidelines are much more likely to contain material that’s worth reading and – most importantly – that’s true. Anything less is a waste of your time.
